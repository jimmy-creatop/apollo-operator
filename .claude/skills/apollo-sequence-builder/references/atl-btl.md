# ATL vs BTL: write to the person, not the company

> Executives and individual contributors read different emails. Decide which one you are writing to before you write a word. ATL is above the line (VP, C-level, director). BTL is below the line (manager, IC, end user). Pull seniority from the list or `profile.yaml`.

## Above the line (VP, C-level, director)

They think strategically, they skim, and they want validation more than a fix.

- **2 to 3 sentences. Maximum.** Every word earns its place.
- **Strategic language:** revenue impact, risk, competitive position, a board-level priority.
- **Lead with the outcome, not the process.** They care what changes, not how it works.
- **A question often lands well:** "What would it mean for next year's targets if..."
- **Fatal mistake:** talking about daily operations, time saved, or manual workflows. It gets you delegated down to a subordinate, and you lose the thread.

## Below the line (manager, IC, end user)

They think tactically, they want a problem solved now, and they care about their daily work.

- **3 to 4 sentences.** A little more room than ATL, still tight.
- **Operational language:** "stop spending 3 hours on...", "cut reporting time by...", specific time or effort saved.
- **Lead with the daily pain.** Name the frustration they actually deal with.
- **Make them look good to their boss.** BTL buyers care how they are perceived.
- **Fatal mistake:** ROI, strategy, and board-level abstractions. Too far from their day, so it gets ignored.

## How to pick

- The list has seniority. VP and above, or "director," go ATL. Manager and below go BTL.
- When a title is ambiguous (a "Head of X" at a 20-person company is often the strategic buyer and the operator both), write closer to ATL but keep one concrete operational proof point.
- In a buying committee you may run both, a strategic angle to the exec and an operational angle to the champion, as separate sequences. Keep them distinct, not a blend that fits neither.
