# Cold email frameworks: the angle menu

> Pick one angle per email, a different one each step. These are proven structures, not fill-in-the-blank templates. The framework gives you the shape; the research and the offer give you the words.

Before you write, ask the three questions (Eric Nowoslawski):
1. How can I make this email worth this person's time?
2. How can I say it like a human would say it?
3. What can I say that my competitors cannot?

## The frameworks

**Do the Math (Thibaut Souyris).** Trigger with a number, a short pitch, a back-of-napkin calculation, a soft CTA. Best when the value is quantifiable.
> "Noticed 50+ open roles. At a $30k cost per mishire and a typical 30% early-churn rate, that is real money. We cut that to under 10% for scale-ups. Worth a chat?"

**Ask Before Pitch / Pattern Interrupt (Will Allred).** Open with a genuine question tied to an observation, earn the right to pitch, then a PS with why it is relevant. Best for busy skimmers.

**Short Trigger (Guillermo Blanco).** A relevant trigger, one line of validation plus value, a CTA. Under 40 words. Best when you have a sharp, specific observation.

**Challenge of Similar Companies (Patrick Trumpi).** Name a challenge companies like theirs face, your solution, a soft CTA, an optional light PS. Best when you sell to a clear vertical.

**Neutral Insight (Chelsea Castle).** Share a genuinely useful third-party resource, explain why it is relevant to them, tie it to their goals. Builds trust first, sells later.

**Upfront Value (Jordan Crawford).** Lead with a useful free thing ("here is a list of every company that just did X, hope it helps"), no ask. The value is the message.

**Leverage content in outbound (Ethan Parker).** Offer a specific, relevant asset ("a cheat sheet on how teams like yours are doing X"), ask if you can send it, PS with why it is relevant now.

**Not Too Different Persona.** "I work with [people like them], they tell me they struggle with [specific pain]." Peer proof. Good when you have strong lookalike social proof.

**Manual Email Style (Josh Braun).** Casual, human, low-pressure, lowers the perceived risk of replying ("not sure it's a fit, but..."). Reads like a real person typed it, because one did.

## The rules every framework obeys

- Be specific and crispy. Vague equals ignorable. "47% fewer bounces" beats "improved deliverability."
- Focus on what sucks about how they do it today, not on your feature. A solution has no value without a problem they feel.
- Avoid generic verbs (optimize, streamline, leverage). Everyone uses them, so they disappear.
- One CTA, soft. Lower the risk of a reply, do not raise the stakes of a meeting.
